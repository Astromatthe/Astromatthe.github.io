  4. S.E.L.E.N.E. – A Lunar Base Design Proposal by 2050
    Giacomo D’Amico, Patrick Grove, Thomas Rimbot, and 8 more authors
    In 54th International Conference on Environmental Systems, Jul 2025
    Abs DOI

    In the context of NASA’s Artemis program, the establishment of a permanent human presence on the Moon is increasingly seen as an inevitable step toward humanity’s expansion beyond Earth. However, the lunar environment presents formidable challenges, including high radiation levels and the logistical and financial burdens of transporting resources from Earth. A self-sufficient lunar base is therefore critical—not only to enable long-term lunar exploration but also to serve as a foundation for future missions to Mars and beyond. As part of the Space Station Design Workshop (SSDW) 2024, this paper presents the conceptual design developed by Team Weiss: S.E.L.E.N.E. (Sustainable Ecosystem with a Large-scale Enterprise for Next-generation Exploration). The mission aims to establish a fully operational, permanently crewed lunar base by 2070, supporting a broader lunar infrastructure and economy. The proposed base comprises both aboveground and underground settlements, a large-scale In Situ Resource Utilization (ISRU) facility for processing lunar regolith, and multiple landing and launch pads. This paper adopts a holistic approach to lunar development, with a particular focus on the design of aboveground deployable inflatable modules. These modules aim to balance engineering subsystems while maintaining a human-centered approach to interior layouts. The housing and research modules will be detailed, including design decisions related to interior spatial organization, structural and energy performance, radiation shielding, and modularity. The overarching goal is to create safe, functional environments that meet both operational requirements and the psycho-physical needs of the crew. Different hypotheses for research module configurations will also be presented, along with the projected role and potential evolution of ISRU capabilities in supporting long-term base operations.

  5. Magnetohydrodynamic Vortex Photobioreactor Concept for Microalgae Cultivation in Space
    Matthias Kura, Gisela Detrell, and Álvaro Romero-Calvo
    In 54th International Conference on Environmental Systems, Jul 2025
    Abs DOI

    With increasing durations of upcoming crewed space missions, bioregenerative life support systems based on microalgae are currently being researched for air revitalization, food production, as well as waste and water treatment. Under microgravity conditions, microalgae have been cultivated in closed-loop pump-driven photobioreactors that use membranes for phase separation. The specific gas diffusion rate, biofilm buildup, and algae damage by mechanical pumping pose limitations in these systems. This paper introduces a novel concept for a magnetohydrodynamically driven photobioreactor for microalgae cultivation. When exposed to a magnetic and perpendicular electric field, a Lorentz force can be introduced in the conductive algae-compatible nutrient solution. When the consequent flow is directed in a circular manner, a vortex is created, mixing the microalgae and acting as a phase separator, driving the liquid outwards and bubbles toward the inside. In- and outlets are required for water and nutrient supply and harvesting. The inlet may also be used to introduce additional gas. An analytical 2D axisymmetric model of the fluid vortex combined with numerical simulations on the flow field within a small-scale reactor demonstrator is presented for the Magnetohydrodynamic Vortex-Inducing Photobioreactor Experiment (MVIPER), scheduled to fly on a parabolic flight in September 2025 as part of the ESA Academy Experiments Programme 2024-2025. The results from the analytical and numerical models show good agreement over a large range of geometric and electrical parameters. Overall, this photobioreactor concept operates with minimal active parts and is expected to significantly reduce the weight of the overall system and required spare parts to improve reliability.
